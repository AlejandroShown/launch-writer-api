# Before/After Copy Training

This file teaches every writing and manager agent what BAD copy looks like (before) vs what GOOD copy looks like (after). When writing or reviewing copy, ask: "Does mine look like a BEFORE or an AFTER?" If it resembles a before, burn it down and rewrite.

---

## PATTERN 1: Abstract/Philosophical vs. Concrete/Demonstrable

### BEFORE (Contra — BAD):
"Introducing Contra Payments, the world's first agentic collaboration layer for creativity. In the past, creative direction was dictated top down, human to human… to sometimes groups of humans. Today, everything is changing. The next wave of creative collaboration will be human to agent to human to agent to… you get it."

**Why it fails:** "Agentic collaboration layer for creativity" means nothing to the viewer. "Human to agent to human" is abstract philosophy. Nobody screenshots this. Nobody understands what the product actually DOES.

### AFTER (Contra — GOOD):
"Introducing Contra Payments, the world's first payment platform that allows you to sell to AI agents. Over $200M has already been paid out to creatives on Contra from humans. And now billions will be paid to creatives by humans and their agents. Let me show you how it works."

**Why it works:** "Sell to AI agents" — you INSTANTLY understand what this does. "$200M already paid" = proof. "Billions will be paid" = scale of opportunity. Concrete. Demonstrable. You can PICTURE it.

### THE RULE: If you can't picture it happening on a screen, rewrite it.

## PATTERN 2: Over-Explained Demo vs. Narrated Screen Recording

### BEFORE (Durable — BAD):
"On Squarespace, you'd spend weeks dragging boxes around a template that looks like everyone else's. Open Durable—type 'mobile detailing in Dallas' and watch. Durable's AI scans the web, writes your copy, generates original photos with NanoBanana—our proprietary image engine—and designs a complete website. Not a template. Built from scratch, unique to your business, live in 30 seconds."

**Why it fails:** "Dragging boxes around a template" — attacking competitors in a way that feels like a pitch, not a demo. "NanoBanana—our proprietary image engine" — nobody cares about the engine name in a launch video. The copy TELLS you what happens instead of SHOWING it happening step by step.

### AFTER (Durable — GOOD):
"We'll tell Durable: 'Build me a car detailing business.' Our AI gets to work. It knows you need a services page, a booking form, and phenomenal imagery for the highest conversion rate. In 30 seconds, you'll get a beautifully designed website optimized for SEO, and full branding ready to launch."

**Why it works:** "We'll tell Durable:" — sacred flow starter, narrating what we DO. "Our AI gets to work" — you see it happening. "It knows you need" — the AI is thinking, deciding. Each sentence is a STEP you can see on screen. No pitch language. Just narration.

### THE RULE: Narrate the screen. "We'll click...", "Right now, [Product] is...", "Here, it notices..."

## PATTERN 3: Jargon-Heavy Technical vs. Expert-Clear Technical

### BEFORE (DepthFirst — BAD):
"DepthFirst deploys AI agents across your entire codebase — right now, they're reasoning through 2 million lines of code, not pattern-matching like Snyk or Veracode, but understanding how your application thinks. Here, it spots a SQL injection buried three layers deep in your payment module — a path no scanner would trace because the input gets sanitized twice before hitting the vulnerable function."

**Why it fails:** "Reasoning through" vs "pattern-matching" is an AI architecture debate — viewers don't care. "SQL injection buried three layers deep" + "input gets sanitized twice before hitting the vulnerable function" = lost everyone who isn't a security engineer. The competitor attack ("not like Snyk or Veracode") feels like a sales pitch, not a demo.

### AFTER (DepthFirst — GOOD):
"First, we'll connect DepthFirst to your source code. Right now, specialized AI agents running our proprietary security models are scanning every line of your code. They understand how your entire system works and show you where you're most vulnerable to attackers. Here, depthfirst flags a critical issue: attackers can exploit a bug in your login page to steal customer data. If left unchecked, this could expose your customers' social security numbers, credit cards, and bank logins."

**Why it works:** "Scanning every line of your code" — clear action. "Login page" — everyone has one, everyone understands. "Social security numbers, credit cards, bank logins" — SPECIFIC scary outcomes anyone understands. No jargon debates. The threat feels REAL and PERSONAL.

### THE RULE: Be the expert who explains it to a smart friend, not the expert who shows off to peers.

## PATTERN 4: Educational/Step-by-Step vs. Momentum-Driven Demo

### BEFORE (Rokt — BAD):
"A customer places an order. Right after they check out, on the thank-you page, Rokt shows them a highly relevant offer from a brand in its ad network. That could be an offer from a brand like Fabletics, Hulu, or Spotify. When the shopper clicks that offer, the advertiser pays for that placement. And you get paid. So instead of your thank-you page doing nothing… …it becomes a new profit center."

**Why it fails:** Reads like a tutorial. "A customer places an order" → "Right after they check out" → "Rokt shows" → "When the shopper clicks" → "And you get paid" — this is an explainer video, not a launch demo. There's no energy, no momentum, no weapons.

### AFTER (Rokt — GOOD):
"Let's say you run an ecommerce brand that wants more profit. In one click you enable Rokt Thanks: a small ad placement that goes on the confirmation page. Let's watch a real customer place an order. Right after they check out, on the thank-you page, Rokt shows them a highly relevant offer. Rokt Brain is optimizing what each shopper sees based on who they are, where they're buying, and billions of past transactions."

**Why it works:** "In one click you enable" — speed + simplicity. "Let's watch a real customer" — shifts to present-tense observation. "Rokt Brain" — the AI has a name and is actively working. "Billions of past transactions" — scale flex. Momentum carries forward.

### THE RULE: The viewer should feel like they're watching something HAPPEN, not reading a manual.

## PATTERN 5: Missing Weapons/Controversy vs. Embedded Weapons

### BEFORE (DepthFirst — BAD):
"Your Veracode dumps 1,600 findings on your engineers — 70% false positives. They stopped reading alerts months ago. The boy who cried wolf, except the wolf is probably fake too."

**Why it fails:** The analogy ("boy who cried wolf") is clever writing but not a weapon. It's a creative writing flex, not something that makes you screenshot and share. And it's attacking competitors by name in a way that feels like a slide deck comparison.

### AFTER (DepthFirst — GOOD):
"Attackers are using AI to break into your systems 10x faster and 10x cheaper than ever before. We are committed to leveling the playing field and protecting companies with AI."

**Why it works:** The enemy isn't a competitor product — it's ATTACKERS using AI. "10x faster and 10x cheaper" = specific, scary multipliers. "Leveling the playing field" = mission alignment weapon. This is a screenshot moment.

### THE RULE: Weapons attack ENEMIES (incumbents, systemic wrongs, threats), not competitor products by name. Unless the competitor IS the enemy.

## PATTERN 6: Hooks — Clever vs. Bold

### BEFORE (DepthFirst — BAD):
"Attackers break into your network in 29 minutes. Your team takes 65 days to patch the hole they used. We raised $40M to close that gap."

**Why it fails:** The stat is interesting but the framing is analytical. "Close that gap" is a soft verb. Where's the "world's first"? Where's the violent verb?

### AFTER (DepthFirst — GOOD):
"We raised an $80M Series B led by Meritech to secure the world's software before it's too late. Introducing depthfirst: the world's first security system built for the age of AI."

**Why it works:** "$80M Series B led by Meritech" = specific credibility. "Before it's too late" = urgency/stakes. "World's first security system built for the age of AI" = category-creating claim. Standard raise + world's first template, executed perfectly.

### BEFORE (Durable — BAD):
"We built the first AI website builder a month before ChatGPT existed. 3 million businesses later, we're back."

**Why it fails:** "A month before ChatGPT existed" is a trivia fact, not a weapon. "We're back" — were they gone? Confusing. No violent verb. No enemy.

### AFTER (Durable — GOOD):
"We raised $20M dollars to kill the 9-5. Introducing Durable. The first AI business builder."

**Why it works:** "Kill the 9-5" — the enemy is a concept EVERYONE understands. Three sentences. Brutal. Clean.

### THE RULE: Bold > clever. Specific > interesting. Short > long.

## PATTERN 7: Weak Positioning vs. Bold Positioning (Rokt)

### BEFORE (Rokt — BAD):
"Introducing Rokt Thanks — the easiest way for ecommerce brands to add meaningful incremental profit with a single placement after checkout."

**Why it fails:** "Easiest way" is generic. "Meaningful incremental profit" is corporate fluff. "Single placement after checkout" is a feature description, not a bold claim. No one screenshots "meaningful incremental profit."

### AFTER (Rokt — GOOD):
"Introducing Rokt Thanks — the first way in the world for a brand to add 10-15% in profit with one click."

**Why it works:** "First way in the world" = category-creating "world's first." "10-15% in profit" = specific number. "One click" = simplicity weapon. Every word is specific and bold.

### THE RULE: "World's first [specific outcome with number]" > "Easiest way to [vague benefit]"

---

## PATTERN 8: AI Slop Comparison vs. Bold Differentiator (Moda)

### BEFORE pattern (generic AI tool):
"Our AI design tool creates beautiful presentations using advanced machine learning. Unlike competitors, our output is fully editable."

**Why it fails:** "Advanced machine learning" = means nothing. "Unlike competitors" = unnamed shade with no punch. "Fully editable" stated as a feature, not a weapon.

### AFTER (Moda — GOOD):
"Every other AI design tool creates AI slop. We built Moda differently. Moda is not a ChatGPT wrapper. We built a Figma-level design canvas first. Then we trained our Agents on typography, layout, and color systems. We taught them taste."

**Why it works:** "AI slop" = 2-word kill shot dismissing ALL competitors. "ChatGPT wrapper" = names the thing everyone's thinking. "Figma-level" = specific quality benchmark. "We taught them taste" = devastating 4-word closer that implies everyone else's AI has no taste.

### THE RULE: Name what competitors ARE (ChatGPT wrapper, AI slop) instead of saying what you're NOT. Then state what makes you different in the most specific terms possible.

---

## SUMMARY: Before vs. After Checklist

When reviewing your own copy, check:

| Before Pattern (KILL) | After Pattern (WRITE) |
|---|---|
| Abstract/philosophical framing | Concrete, demonstrable action |
| Telling what the product does | Narrating what you see on screen |
| Jargon showing off to peers | Expert explaining to smart friend |
| Tutorial/educational tone | Momentum-driven demo |
| Clever analogies | Bold, quotable weapons |
| Competitor attacks by product name | Enemy attacks on systemic problems |
| Soft verbs (build, improve, close) | Violent verbs (kill, replace, destroy) |
| Long explanations | Short declarations |
| "We built X that does Y and Z" | "We raised $X to kill Y. Introducing Z." |
